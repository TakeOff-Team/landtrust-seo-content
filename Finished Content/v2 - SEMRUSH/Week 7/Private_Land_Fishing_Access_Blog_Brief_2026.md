### **2. Do You Need a Fishing License on Private Water? (350–400 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "do you need a fishing license to fish private water," "can you fish private pond without a license," "private water fishing license rules"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (75 words):**
- This is the most-searched question in the private water fishing category — and the answer is more nuanced than most people expect
- The general rule: most states require a valid fishing license regardless of whether you're fishing public or private water — unless the pond is entirely enclosed on your own property and you are the property owner

**State-by-State Overview (150 words):**
- **Texas:** A fishing license is required on all private water unless the water body is entirely within a private property and is not accessible to navigable waterways. Even on private ranch ponds, non-landowners typically need a license.
- **Pennsylvania:** PA requires a fishing license for all ages 16+ regardless of water ownership. Private pond exemptions exist only for the landowner and immediate family members.
- **Montana:** License required for all anglers 12+ on any water, including private streams. Landowner access doesn't exempt visiting anglers.
- **General rule:** When booking through LandTrust, bring your fishing license. The access permission from the landowner covers trespassing law — a fishing license covers your right to fish.
- Note: Some private stocked put-and-take ponds sell their own day permits that fulfill state license requirements — LandTrust listings will note if this applies.

**What Private Access DOES Change (75 words):**
- Trespass law: you have legal right to be on the property
- Crowd pressure: effectively zero
- Gear and method restrictions: often relaxed compared to state-managed fisheries (varies by listing and state)
- Catch limits: some private water has no limits; others set limits to protect fish populations — always confirm with the landowner before your trip

**Farm Ponds and Stock Tanks (100 words):**
- The most common private water type; found across the Midwest, South, and Plains states
- Primary species: largemouth bass, bluegill, catfish; occasional crappie and hybrid striped bass
- Key advantage: farm ponds are rarely fished hard; landowners often stock them regularly and manage for size

**Private Trout Streams and Spring Creeks (125 words):**

- Private river bottom properties give exclusive bank or wade access to sections of productive rivers without guide fees
- Key tip: Montana and Wyoming spring creeks are often catch-and-release only — verify before booking if you want to keep fish
