### **1. What Is CWD — And Why 2026 Is a Critical Year (350 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "chronic wasting disease deer," "cwd deer," "cwd disease"

**Content Structure:**

**The disease at a glance (100 words):**
- CWD is a fatal prion disease affecting deer, elk, moose, and other cervids — no cure, no vaccine
- Prions are misfolded proteins that destroy brain and nervous tissue over 18+ months before symptoms appear
- Infected animals shed prions through saliva, urine, feces — contaminating soil and water; prions persist for decades
- As of early 2026: confirmed in 36+ U.S. states, 5 Canadian provinces, and South Korea

**2026 spread update (125 words):**
- Delaware confirmed its first case (April 2026) — CWD is no longer a "western problem"
- Pennsylvania: positive cases at Gettysburg National Military Park, 2026
- Nebraska: 197 positives detected in the 2025 deer season surveillance alone
- Montana: prevalence reaching 39% in whitetail deer in some drainages — nearly 4 in 10 animals
- States actively managing new detections: Louisiana, Virginia, North Carolina, Missouri
- Interactive CWD map note: direct readers to USGS or CWD-INFO.ORG for current state-by-state status

### **2. What Is EHD — The Other Disease Deer Hunters Need to Know (300 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "epizootic hemorrhagic disease deer," "deer disease," "ehd deer"

**Content Structure:**

**EHD basics (100 words):**
- Epizootic Hemorrhagic Disease (EHD) is a viral disease spread by biting midges (Culicoides species), NOT by direct deer-to-deer contact
- EHD cannot spread to humans — hunters who find dead deer near water are not at risk
- Symptoms: high fever, weakness, excessive salivation, deer seeking water/streams, swelling of head and neck, bluish tongue ("bluetongue" variant)
- Death typically occurs within 5–10 days of infection; some deer survive and develop immunity
- Herd recovery usually takes 2–3 years after an outbreak, depending on severity

**Seasonality and spread (100 words):**
- EHD peaks August–September during hot, dry conditions when midge populations surge
- The disease ends abruptly with the first hard frost that kills the midge vector
- Most common in Midwest and Southeast; drought years are the worst — hot summers mean more midges, more infected deer
- 2025–2026 EHD activity: elevated in Missouri, Indiana, and parts of the Great Plains following a dry late summer

[QUOTE: Landowner or hunt club manager who found dead deer near a water source during EHD season — what they thought it was, how they confirmed it, and what they did next]

**What hunters should do when they find a dead deer near water (100 words):**
- Don't assume it's CWD — if it's August or September and the deer is near a creek, EHD is the more likely culprit
- Report to your state wildlife agency with GPS coordinates and photos — biologist data collection matters
- Do not consume meat from visibly sick or recently deceased animals
- EHD does NOT affect meat safety in animals that were harvested healthy — deer that survived a prior season's EHD exposure are fine

### **5. Meat Safety and Processing: What You Need to Know Before You Eat (275 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "chronic wasting disease deer meat," "can you eat a deer with cwd," "wasting disease in humans"

**Content Structure:**

**The human health question (100 words):**
- CWD has NOT been shown to infect humans — as of 2026, no confirmed case of CWD transmission to humans exists
- However: the CDC strongly advises against eating meat from confirmed CWD-positive animals, citing the precautionary principle; prion diseases in other animals have historically surprised scientists
- Key tissues to avoid in any deer from a CWD-prevalent area: brain, spinal cord, eyes, spleen, lymph nodes — these concentrate prion proteins
- EHD poses zero human health risk — it is a deer-specific virus

**Safe processing protocols (100 words):**
- If hunting in a CWD-known zone, submit your deer for testing before processing if state offers the service; many states hold samples for you
- Deboning technique: remove all meat from the bone without cutting through the spinal column; keeps muscle tissue (the safest part) separate from high-risk tissue
- Wear latex or rubber gloves during field dressing; wash hands and tools thoroughly after
- If the test comes back positive: do not consume; follow state guidance for proper carcass disposal
