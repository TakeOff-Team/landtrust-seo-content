**What Residents Need (125 words):**
- Georgia Hunting License: $15/year (required base for all hunting)
- Georgia Migratory Bird License: $5/year (mandatory for dove, teal, woodcock, and other migratory birds)
- Federal HIP Registration: Free — Harvest Information Program registration, required for all migratory bird hunters; done online or through a license vendor in minutes
- Hunter Education Certificate: Required if born on or after January 1, 1961; one-time completion, good for life

- Federal Duck Stamp ($27): Required if you're 16+ and plan to hunt *any* migratory waterfowl — not required for dove-only hunts, but worth noting for hunters combining dove and teal seasons
- Non-residents are increasingly booking Georgia dove hunts as destination hunts — the $100 annual license makes multiple trips worthwhile

**Age and Hunter Education (75 words):**
- Hunters under 16: Can hunt without a hunting license if accompanied by a licensed adult
- Hunter education: Required for hunters born after January 1, 1961; courses available online through georgiawildlife.com or hunter-ed.com

### **3. Bag Limits, Legal Methods, and Field Rules (325 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "dove hunting regulations georgia," "georgia dove bag limit," "dove hunting season georgia"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (40 words):**
- Georgia follows federal migratory bird frameworks for dove hunting rules, which means some of the legal limits and shooting restrictions are set in Washington, not Atlanta. Here's what you need to know before you hit the field.

**Bag and Possession Limits (75 words):**
- Daily bag limit: 15 mourning doves
- Possession limit: 45 (three times the daily bag — standard federal formula)
- Eurasian Collared Dove: Year-round season, no bag limit — note that wing attached (proof of species) must be retained if mixing with mourning doves
- White-winged dove: Same 15-bird combined bag limit as mourning dove

**Legal Shooting Methods (75 words):**
- Shotguns only — rifles and pistols prohibited for migratory birds
- Shotgun must be plugged to hold no more than three shells total (magazine + chamber)
- Shot size: #2 or smaller
- Non-toxic shot: Not currently mandated for doves in Georgia on most fields (unlike waterfowl), but recommended on some managed fields — verify specific field rules before hunting

**Baiting Rules (100 words):**
- Baiting dove fields is a federal violation — one of the most enforced wildlife laws in the South
- Legal field preparation: Normal agricultural crop rotation (millet, sunflowers, corn, sorghum) is legal; intentionally scattering grain to attract birds is not
- "Top-sowed" fields planted specifically to provide standing food sources are legal if part of a legitimate farming operation
- The baiting prohibition extends to hunting within 10 days of bait removal
- When in doubt: If a landowner can't explain the agricultural purpose of the field, walk away — the legal risk lands on the hunter

**GADNR Public Dove Fields (125 words):**
- Georgia manages approximately 60+ public dove fields statewide through the Wildlife Resources Division

### **5. Georgia Dove Hunting: Scouting, Gear, and Strategy (375 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "dove season georgia," "dove hunts in georgia," "late season dove hunting georgia"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Even with the right field and the right license, doves can humble you fast. They're small, fast, and erratic — and the hunters who consistently bag out are the ones who put in pre-season work and know what to adjust when the birds change their patterns.

**Scouting (100 words):**
- Food sources drive dove movement: actively harvested fields, standing millet, sunflowers, corn stubble, and water sources in dry Septembers
- Look for fresh combine activity and bare-soil areas — freshly disked fields hold birds at dawn as they scratch for seed
- Flight corridors: Birds funnel predictably along treelines, power lines, and field edges connecting roost to feed areas; identify these in advance and set up on the funnel, not in the middle of the field
- Late-season scouting (December–January): Birds shift to roost-edge habitats as food sources thin — locate roosts using morning/evening flyways and set up on the exit routes

**Gear Essentials (100 words):**
- Shotgun: 12 or 20 gauge with modified or improved-modified choke; most shots are in the 20–40 yard range
- Shells: 7½ or 8 shot in 2¾" loads; lighter loads reduce fatigue on high-volume shooting days
- Hunting seat/stool: Stand-and-shoot style hunting wears you out quickly; a low-profile stool keeps you comfortable during slow periods
- Dove vest with large game pouch: Practical for carrying shells and birds; easy to forget until you're stuffing doves into your jacket pockets
- Decoys: 5–8 dove decoys (spinning-wing or static) placed on a dead snag or fence post near your setup increases pull from passing flocks

**Shooting Tips (100 words):**
- Lead the bird — mourning doves fly 35–55 mph and require substantial forward lead, especially on crossing shots
- Keep the gun moving through the swing; stopping the gun is the fastest way to miss
- Morning and late-afternoon peak: Birds move most actively in the two hours after sunrise and the two hours before sunset; midday is the slow period
- Small groups shoot better than large crowds — five guns spread 75 yards apart will consistently outperform fifteen guns bunched at one end of a field
