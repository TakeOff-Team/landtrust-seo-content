### **1. Maryland Hunting Licenses and Fees: What You Need Before Opening Day (400 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "maryland hunting regulations," "maryland hunting license," "md hunting regulations," "maryland hunting license cost"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Every Maryland hunter — resident or non-resident, whether hunting their own land or public WMAs — needs a valid hunting license. The good news: licenses are available online through Maryland's Compass system. The less-good news: the fee structure has multiple layers depending on species, weapon, and residency status.

**License Types and Costs (150 words):**
- Resident Regular Hunting License: $24.50
- Non-Resident Hunting License: $130.00
- Junior licenses available for hunters under 16 (resident $10.50, non-resident $32.50)
- Senior licenses for 65+ (resident $5.00, non-resident $65.00)
- Apprentice hunting license (with mentor, no prior education required): $10.00 for residents
- All first-time hunters must complete a hunter education course before getting a license — MD uses the official hunter-ed.com certification process
- Stamps and permits stack on top of the base license: HIP (Harvest Information Program) registration required for migratory birds; federal duck stamp required for waterfowl; deer stamp required for deer hunting
- Table recommended: License Type | Resident Cost | Non-Resident Cost

**What Non-Residents Actually Pay for a Full Season (100 words):**
- Non-resident planning a deer + waterfowl trip: $130 base license + deer stamp + HIP registration + federal duck stamp ($27) — budget $175–$200 before licenses are in hand
- Non-resident deer-only hunter: $130 base + deer stamp — approximately $140–$155 total
- Context: Compared to western states with trophy big game, Maryland's non-resident fees are reasonable for the experience available — especially given the sika deer opportunity, which is genuinely rare in North America

**Buying Licenses (75 words):**
- Maryland Compass system (compass.dnr.maryland.gov) — online license purchase and management
- All sales final — no refunds for game not found or seasons that end differently than planned
- License year runs from specific dates tied to season type — confirm before purchasing out of season
- Paper licenses may be required for specific activities — check DNR guidance annually

**Pro Tip (25 words):**
"Buy your HIP registration at the same time as your hunting license — it's free and required for any migratory birds. Forgetting it means a separate trip back to buy it before you can legally hunt doves or ducks."

### **2. Maryland Deer Seasons: Archery, Muzzleloader, Firearms, and Sika (450 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "deer hunting in md," "deer season in maryland," "maryland archery season," "maryland muzzleloader season," "maryland sika deer hunting," "maryland deer hunting regulations," "sunday deer hunting in maryland"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Maryland deer hunting runs from early September through late January, with multiple weapon seasons staggered across the calendar. The key thing to understand before you plan your hunt: which region you're in determines your bag limits and specific dates. Region A covers the western part of the state; Region B covers the Eastern Shore.

**Season Dates Overview (125 words):**
- Archery (antlered white-tail): September 5 – October 15; October 19 – November 28; December 15–19; January 4–8; January 12–31 (with Region A exceptions for specific dates)
- Muzzleloader: October 16–18 (statewide); December 20 – January 3; Region B adds October 20–25 for antlerless
- Firearms (antlered): November 29 – December 13; January 9–11 (Region A)
- Firearms (antlerless, all lands): December 6–13
- Primitive Hunt Days: February 2–4 (long bows, recurves, flintlock only)
- Sunday deer hunting: generally included in white-tailed and sika deer seasons statewide, but individual WMAs and some counties prohibit Sunday hunting — verify before heading out
- Table format recommended: Season | Open Dates | Region A Bag Limit | Region B Bag Limit

**Bag Limits by Region (75 words):**
- Region A (Western MD): 2 antlered total for all seasons combined; up to 2 antlerless per weapon season (no more than 2 total all seasons)
- Region B (Eastern Shore): 2 antlered total; 15 antlerless during archery, 10 during muzzleloader, 10 during firearms — aggressive antlerless management to support population control in high-density ag country
- Junior hunt days: November 16 statewide; November 17 in certain counties; special bag limits apply

**Sika Deer: Maryland's Bucket-List Hunt (150 words):**
- Maryland's Eastern Shore — primarily Dorchester County and surrounding marsh country — holds one of the only free-ranging wild sika deer populations in North America; the other notable population is in Virginia's Chincoteague area
- Sika are a non-native Asian elk introduced to Assateague Island in the 1910s; they've thrived in Maryland's tidal marshes and managed to spread throughout the lower Eastern Shore
- Season dates parallel white-tail on the Eastern Shore; sika are typically pursued in thick marsh grass, requiring different tactics than open-country white-tail hunting
- Bag limit: 3 sika total, no more than 1 antlered
- This hunt draws out-of-state hunters specifically for the experience — private marsh access through LandTrust is a significant advantage on the Eastern Shore where public access is limited and pressure on WMAs can be intense

**Pro Tip (50 words):**
"If you're planning to hunt sika, focus your access on private marsh ground. WMA pressure on the Eastern Shore during firearms season is significant, and sika are masters at hiding in cordgrass and phragmites — pressure pushes them deep into private marsh that most hunters can't access."

**Target Keywords:** "maryland waterfowl hunting," "duck hunting season maryland," "maryland goose season," "maryland waterfowl season," "md duck hunting season," "maryland waterfowl regulations"

**Opening (50 words):**
- Maryland sits at the heart of the Atlantic Flyway, and the Chesapeake Bay is arguably the best waterfowl destination on the entire East Coast. Canada geese by the hundreds of thousands funnel into the Chesapeake corridor each fall. Mallards, black ducks, diving ducks, and teal follow. For waterfowlers, Maryland is a serious destination.

**Why Maryland (100 words):**
- The Chesapeake Bay's mix of open water, tidal marshes, agricultural fields, and river backwaters creates habitat diversity that supports nearly every Atlantic Flyway species
- Canada geese: both resident Canada geese (AF Resident Population) and migratory Atlantic Population geese funnel through; regulations differ significantly between the two — know which population you're targeting
- Eastern Zone vs. Western Zone split waterfowl seasons — Eastern Zone (Eastern Shore and Bay) typically opens earlier and later than Western Zone; confirm annual dates as federal frameworks shift year to year
- Mixed-bag opportunities: a single Maryland marsh hunt can produce mallards, black ducks, Canada geese, teal, and bufflehead in one morning

**Season Dates by Species (150 words):**
- Special Early Resident Canada Geese: September 1–15 (Eastern Zone); September 1–25 (Western Zone)
- September Teal: September 18–27
- Ducks, Coot, Merganser: Eastern Zone — October 11–18, November 15–28, December 16 – January 31; Western Zone — October 3–18, November 22–28, December 16 – January 31
- Canada Geese (AF Resident Population): November 22–28; December 15 – March 7
- Atlantic Population Canada Geese: December 19 – January 3; January 14–31 (very limited season — 1 bird/day limit)
- Light Geese: October 1 – November 28; December 15 – January 31; plus Conservation Order extends into spring
- Mourning Dove: September 1 – October 18; October 25 – November 28; December 20 – January 9
- HIP registration and federal duck stamp required for all migratory birds — purchase before season opens

**Private Land Advantage for Waterfowl (75 words):**
- Maryland's WMAs and public blinds are lottery-based or heavily competed — especially prime Chesapeake Bay spots; private marsh and field access removes this bottleneck

### **5. Where to Hunt in Maryland: Public Land, WMAs, and Private Access (375 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "hunting calendar maryland," "maryland hunting regions map," "md dnr hunting seasons," "hunting in maryland"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Maryland is a small state with a lot of hunters and comparatively limited public ground. That math creates access competition on WMAs, especially during peak firearms deer season and prime waterfowl dates. Understanding where to hunt — and when private access solves public land crowding — is as important as knowing the season dates.

**Public Land Overview (125 words):**
- Maryland's Wildlife & Heritage Service maintains 64 Wildlife Management Areas (WMAs) totaling approximately 128,000 acres across 19 of Maryland's 23 counties
- Additional hunting on some state parks (~4,000 acres of huntable land) with species and method restrictions that vary by park
- WMA quality varies significantly: some western Maryland WMAs hold excellent deer and turkey habitat; Eastern Shore WMAs offer waterfowl and sika opportunities but face intense pressure during peak seasons
- State land hunting is free with a valid hunting license — no additional fees beyond license requirements
- Sunday hunting is not permitted on all WMAs — check individual WMA regulations before planning a Sunday hunt; this is where most hunters get caught off guard

**Private Land Access (150 words):**
- Maryland's density problem is real: 6+ million residents, 128,000 WMA acres, and a hunting culture that's grown significantly in the past decade mean good public spots are competitive
- Private farm fields, Eastern Shore marshes, and western Maryland ridge properties represent the quality hunting ground that most Maryland hunters never access
- LandTrust connects hunters with Maryland landowners who list private access for day hunts and seasonal leases — deer stands in ag country, sika marsh access on the Eastern Shore, dove fields, and Canada goose decoying setups in working grain fields
- Private access removes the early-morning WMA parking lot competition and puts hunters in front of less-pressured deer, sika, and waterfowl
