### **1. Maryland's Two Hunting Regions: What Every Hunter Needs to Know First (400 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "maryland hunting regulations," "maryland hunting seasons," "hunting in maryland season," "maryland hunting regions"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Before you look at a single season date, you need to know which region you're hunting. Maryland divides the state into Region A and Region B — the bag limits, antlerless deer structures, and Sunday hunting rules are materially different. Getting this wrong means either leaving deer on the table or picking up a violation.

**Region A vs. Region B Breakdown (175 words):**
- **Region A:** Allegany, Garrett, and Washington counties — western Maryland mountains and ridgelines; historically lower deer density, tighter antlerless bag limits
- **Region B:** All other Maryland counties — Eastern Shore, Piedmont, and the central farmland belt; higher deer density, more liberal antlerless limits (up to 15 antlerless deer in some seasons in Region B), and the heart of Maryland's sika deer range
- Simple table recommended: Region | Counties | Antlered Limit | Antlerless Limit | Sunday Hunting?
- Note: Some regulations apply statewide regardless of region — always confirm species-specific rules with current DNR guidance

**Sunday Hunting — The Short Version (100 words):**
- Sunday hunting in Maryland is legal but county-dependent. Certain counties allow Sunday hunting with archery during archery season; others prohibit it entirely. Sika deer and white-tailed deer have Sunday seasons in specific counties — check the current DNR Sunday Hunting map before assuming your area is open.
- Key point: Private land doesn't override the Sunday restriction. If your county is closed on Sundays, you can't hunt — regardless of whose land you're on.

### **2. Maryland Deer Seasons: Archery, Firearms, Muzzleloader, and Sika (450 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "deer hunting in maryland," "deer hunting season in maryland," "maryland archery season," "maryland muzzleloader season," "maryland sika deer hunting," "maryland deer hunting regulations"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Maryland's deer season is one of the longest and most layered on the East Coast. Archery runs from early September all the way through January, with firearms and muzzleloader seasons woven in between. If you're hunting both white-tailed and sika, the seasons mostly align — but there are a few critical date differences to know.

**White-Tailed Deer Season Dates (150 words):**
- **Archery (Antlered and Antlerless):** Opens September 5, runs through late January with breaks during late December firearms windows; specific Sunday hunts vary by county
- **Muzzleloader:** October 16–18, 2025 (first segment, statewide); December–January segment follows; antlerless-only and antlered structures differ — confirm per region
- **Firearms:** Opens the Saturday after Thanksgiving (November 29, 2025); runs two weeks through mid-December; second segment in January (Jan. 9–11, 2026)
- **Primitive Hunt Days:** February 2–4 — longbows, recurves, or flintlock/sidelock percussion muzzleloaders only; one additional antlerless deer allowed
- **Junior Deer Hunt Days:** Mid-November statewide; hunters 16 and under
- Bag limits: 2 antlered deer total all seasons combined; antlerless limits vary significantly by region (Region B up to 15 antlerless in select seasons)

**Sika Deer — Maryland's Unique Trophy (125 words):**
- Wild sika deer exist in the United States in only one place: Maryland's Eastern Shore, concentrated in Dorchester County marshes. Sika seasons largely mirror white-tailed deer archery and firearms seasons, with some differences in muzzleloader structure.
- Sika are smaller than whitetails, vocal, and considered a bucket-list hunt by serious deer hunters — the fact that Maryland is the only state offering this hunt is a major draw for out-of-state hunters willing to travel.
- The catch: sika habitat is almost entirely private marshland and agricultural fields. Public WMA access for sika exists but is heavily pressured — private land through LandTrust is how most successful sika hunters secure their spots.

### **3. Waterfowl Hunting in Maryland: Ducks, Geese, and the Atlantic Flyway (400 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "maryland waterfowl hunting," "duck hunting season maryland," "maryland goose season," "maryland waterfowl season," "md duck hunting season"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (60 words):**
- Maryland sits at the confluence of the Chesapeake Bay and the Atlantic Flyway — the primary migration corridor for waterfowl traveling down the Eastern Seaboard. This geography makes Maryland one of the premier waterfowl states on the East Coast. The Chesapeake and its tributaries funnel millions of migrating birds each fall and winter directly over private marshes and agricultural fields.

**Duck Season Structure (150 words):**
- Maryland splits the state into **Eastern Zone** and **Western Zone** for waterfowl, roughly aligning with the Eastern Shore and western/central Maryland
- **September Teal:** September 18–27, 2025 — early teal season, no zone split required
- **Duck, Coot, and Merganser:**
  - Eastern Zone: October 11–18, November 15–28, December 16, 2025–January 31, 2026
  - Western Zone: October 3–18, November 22–28, December 16, 2025–January 31, 2026
- **Black Duck:** Special restrictions within these windows — reduced bag limits and zone-specific rules apply
- Daily bag limit: 6 ducks total (with federal species sub-limits)
- Federal HIP registration and federal Duck Stamp required in addition to state license
- Practical tip: The late December–January peak is when the most birds are in the state; early October teal season is the sleeper opportunity

**Canada Geese and Light Geese (100 words):**
- Maryland is one of the top Canada goose states on the East Coast — the agricultural fields and Chesapeake shorelines are prime wintering grounds for Atlantic Flyway geese
- **Early Resident Canada Geese:** September 1–25 (Western Zone), September 1–15 (Eastern Zone) — targeting resident birds before migration pressure begins
- **Atlantic Population Geese:** December 19, 2025–January 3, 2026 and January 14–31, 2026
- **AF Resident Population:** November 22–March 7 with gaps; highest bag limits of the season
- **Light Geese / Conservation Order:** Extended through April 15; no daily limits during conservation order — Maryland is a prime destination for late-season snow goose hunting

**Turkey (125 words):**
- **Spring Season 2026:** Junior hunt April 11 (statewide) and April 12 (specific counties); general season April 18–May 23
- **Fall Season:** November 1–9, 2025 — short window, both archery and firearms
- **Winter Season:** January 22–24, 2026 — brief late-season opportunity
- Bag limits: Spring — 1 bearded per day, 2 bearded per season; Fall/Winter combined — 1 either sex
- Maryland turkey populations are strongest in the far east (Eastern Shore farmland) and far west (Allegany and Garrett counties); the center of the state is lighter turkey habitat
- A general hunting license is required; no additional turkey stamp needed for spring or fall

**Small Game and Upland Birds (100 words):**
- **Squirrel:** September 6, 2025–February 28, 2026; 6 per day
- **Cottontail Rabbit:** November 1, 2025–February 28, 2026; 4 per day
- **Pheasant:** November 1, 2025–February 28, 2026; 2 per day either sex; limited to stocked areas and some private lands
- **Quail:** November 1, 2025–January 15, 2026; private lands only in most areas; 6 per day
- **Ruffed Grouse:** October 4–December 31, 2025; 2 per day; western Maryland (Region A) only
- **Black Bear:** Brief firearms season (typically 6 days in late October); western Maryland only; limited permits

**Coyote (50 words):**

- Maryland licenses are purchased online through the DNR's Compass system or at licensed vendors statewide

- Resident Senior Hunting License (65+): $5.00
- Resident Apprentice Hunting License: $10.00
