### **1. Maryland Hunting Licenses: Types, Costs, and What You Actually Need (375 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "maryland hunting regulations," "md hunting regulations," "maryland hunting license," "maryland dnr hunting seasons"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Before you buy a tag or load a magazine, you need the right licenses. Maryland's system layers a base hunting license with species-specific stamps and permits — and the requirements differ for residents, non-residents, juniors, and seniors. Getting this right before the season opens saves headaches at check stations.

**License Types and Costs (150 words):**
- Resident hunting license: ~$24.50 per year (adults)
- Non-resident hunting license: ~$130.00 per year
- Junior hunting license (under 16): ~$10.50 resident / ~$32.50 non-resident
- Senior hunting license (65+): ~$5.00 resident / ~$65.00 non-resident
- Apprentice license: ~$10.00 (allows first-time hunters to hunt without completing safety course, under licensed adult supervision)
- Additional stamps required by species: deer stamp, antlerless stamp, turkey license, federal duck stamp + state waterfowl stamp for waterfowl hunters
- Hunter education certification required for all first-time hunters (limited exceptions for military/law enforcement)
- Table format recommended: License Type | Resident Cost | Non-Resident Cost | Notes

**Where to Buy (50 words):**
- Maryland DNR's online COMPASS system (compass.dnr.maryland.gov) — licenses available online, at sporting goods retailers, and at select county DNR offices
- Federal duck stamp required for waterfowl hunters: purchase at USPS offices or online at fws.gov
- Licenses are valid for the Maryland license year (resident year begins July 1)

**Hunter Education (75 words):**
- First-time hunters must complete a Maryland Hunter Safety Course before purchasing a standard license
- Courses available online (with in-person field day) and in-person
- Apprentice license option: allows new hunters to try hunting first under a licensed adult hunter (age 18+); valid for one year; limited to two apprentice licenses per lifetime
- No exemptions for landowners — all hunters need a valid license to hunt in Maryland, even on their own property

**Pro Tip (50 words):**
"If you're hunting deer, waterfowl, AND turkey in Maryland, you'll need three separate licenses/permits beyond the base hunting license. Add up what you actually plan to hunt before purchasing — the total can run $80–$150+ for a full resident season when all stamps are included."

### **2. Maryland Deer Seasons: Archery, Firearms, Muzzleloader, and the Region A/B Divide (425 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "deer hunting season in maryland," "maryland archery season," "maryland muzzleloader season," "deer season in maryland," "maryland deer hunting regulations," "sunday deer hunting in maryland"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- White-tailed deer hunting in Maryland runs from early September through late January — one of the longest combined deer seasons on the East Coast. But the season structure is layered: archery, muzzleloader, firearms, and primitive hunt days each have their own dates, and Region A vs. Region B rules create meaningful bag limit differences.

**Understanding Maryland's Hunting Regions (100 words):**
- **Region A:** Allegany, Garrett, and Washington counties (Western Maryland — mountainous terrain, lower deer density, more restrictive antlerless limits)
  - Antlerless bag limit: up to 2 deer per weapon season, no more than 2 total all seasons combined
- **Region B:** All other Maryland counties (rest of state — agricultural land, higher deer density, more liberal antlerless limits)
  - Antlerless bag limit: up to 15 (archery), 10 (muzzleloader), 10 (firearms)
- Key insight: Region B's liberal antlerless limit reflects deer management needs in high-density agricultural counties; hunters focused on doe management have exceptional opportunity statewide

**2026–2027 Season Dates (150 words):**
- **Archery (white-tail):** September 5 – October 15, October 19 – November 28, December 15–19, January 4–31 (additional Region A dates January 9–11)
- **Muzzleloader:** October 16–18 (first segment, statewide); December 20 – January 3 (second segment); Region B adds October 20–25 for antlerless muzzleloader
- **Firearms (antlered white-tail):** November 29 – December 13 and January 9–11
- **Primitive Hunt Days:** February 2–4 (longbows, recurve bows, flintlock/sidelock muzzleloaders only; one additional antlerless deer may be taken)
- **Antlered bag limit:** 2 total all seasons combined statewide
- **Sunday hunting:** Some counties allow Sunday deer hunting; others restrict it — check the MD DNR county-by-county chart

**Sika Deer (75 words):**
- Sika deer season follows similar dates to white-tail in most weapon categories
- Maryland's Eastern Shore holds North America's only wild sika deer population; Dorchester County WMAs and private marshland are the epicenter
- Bag limit: 3 sika deer total, no more than 1 antlered
- Sika deer are Japanese deer; wary and often nocturnal; hunting them in marshes and thick reed beds is a dramatically different experience from white-tail stand hunting
- Private marsh land access dramatically improves sika hunting success vs. crowded WMAs

**Pro Tip (50 words):**
"Maryland's firearms season opens the Saturday after Thanksgiving — one of the most hunted windows of the year statewide. If you're hunting WMAs that week, expect pressure. Private land hunters consistently report better buck encounters during firearms season simply because they're the only ones in their area."

**The Atlantic Flyway Context (75 words):**

### **4. Maryland Turkey Season: Spring Gobbler, Fall, and Junior Hunt Days (350 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "maryland turkey season," "turkey hunting in maryland," "turkey hunting season maryland"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Turkey hunting in Maryland is a spring tradition for most hunters — the state's spring gobbler season runs from late April through late May and produces consistent action across the western mountains and Southern Maryland woodlands. A fall season adds a second opportunity in November, and a brief winter season rounds out the calendar.

**Spring Turkey Season (125 words):**
- **Junior Turkey Hunt:** April 11 (statewide); April 12 (specific counties, Sunday)
- **General Spring Season:** April 18 – May 23
- Bag limit: 1 bearded turkey per day, 2 bearded per season
- Best areas: Western Maryland (Allegany, Garrett, Washington counties) for mountain gobblers; Southern Maryland and Eastern Shore for agricultural edge birds
- No dog-assisted turkey hunting permitted
- Both archery and firearm methods legal during general season
- Non-resident turkey license required in addition to base hunting license
- Maryland turkey populations are healthy statewide; average harvest runs 3,000–4,000 birds per spring

**Fall and Winter Turkey (75 words):**
- **Fall Season:** November 1–9 (either sex; bag limit 1, combined with winter season)
- **Winter Season:** January 22–24 (either sex; combined bag limit with fall season = 1 turkey total)
- Fall turkey hunting in Maryland allows dog-assisted hunting (certain methods), giving hunters a different tactical approach than spring
- Fall season timing overlaps late archery deer season — combination fall trips hunting turkey in mornings and deer from stands afternoons are popular

**Private Land Advantage (50 words):**
- Spring turkey season success rises sharply on private land where birds haven't been pressured; private timber parcels and agricultural edges in Western Maryland and the Eastern Shore hold birds that WMA hunters rarely encounter
- Private land turkey access through LandTrust is often available as a day-hunt without a multi-day commitment

**Pro Tip (50 words):**

### **5. Small Game, Upland Birds, and Special Regulations (300 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "hunting calendar maryland," "maryland pheasant hunting," "coyote hunting maryland," "md hunting regulations"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (40 words):**
- Maryland's small game and upland bird seasons add texture to a hunting calendar that can otherwise feel like it's all deer and waterfowl. Rabbits, squirrels, pheasant, quail, ruffed grouse, and mourning dove all have huntable seasons — and coyotes are open year-round.

**Small Game Season Dates (125 words):**
- **Cottontail Rabbit:** November 1 – February 28 (4/day, 8 in possession)
- **Squirrel (gray, red, eastern fox):** September 6 – February 28 (6/day, 12 in possession)
- **Ruffed Grouse:** October 4 – December 31 (2/day, 4 in possession)
- **Pheasant:** November 1 – February 28 (2/day either sex, 4 in possession); strongest populations in managed areas and private farms
- **Quail:** November 1 – January 15 (6/day, 12 in possession); some private lands only — wild quail populations are limited; most hunting occurs on stocked private preserves
- **Mourning Dove:** September 1 – October 18, October 25 – November 28, December 20 – January 9 (15/day)
- **Crow:** August 15 – March 15 (no bag limit during open season)
- **Coyote:** Open year-round, no bag limit — can be taken by any legal method during open hunting seasons; no separate license required beyond base hunting license

**Furbearer Seasons (75 words):**
- Black bear: limited archery and muzzleloader bear season in October (Western Maryland, by permit only; check DNR for current quota status)
- Furbearers — raccoon, fox, mink, muskrat, opossum, skunk: regulated trapping seasons October–March
- Bobcat may not be hunted or trapped in Maryland (endangered)
- Fisher: November 1 – February 1
