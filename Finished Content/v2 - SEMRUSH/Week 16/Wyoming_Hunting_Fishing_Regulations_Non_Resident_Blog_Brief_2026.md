### **1. Wyoming Non-Resident Hunting License: Types, Costs, and What You Actually Need (425 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "wyoming hunting license non-resident," "wyoming hunting license types," "wyoming hunting license cost," "wyoming hunting license price"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- The first thing every non-resident needs to understand: Wyoming has tiered license types, and the costs between resident and non-resident are substantial — knowing which licenses you need before applying saves confusion and missed deadlines.

**License Types Breakdown (175 words):**
- Conservation stamp (required for all license holders)
- General non-resident hunting license (required as a base for all hunting)
- Species-specific licenses: deer, elk, antelope (separate for each)
- Preference point system for limited quota species
- Small game license (cheaper, no draw required for most species)
- Combination hunting/fishing licenses available
- Table format recommended: License Type | Resident Cost | Non-Resident Cost | Draw Required?

**The Mandatory Outfitter Rule (100 words):**
- Non-resident big game and trophy game hunters in any federally designated wilderness area must hunt with a licensed Wyoming outfitter or resident guide
- Wyoming Board of Outfitters and Professional Guides licenses all outfitters — verify before booking
- **The private land exception:** Private land hunts outside designated wilderness do not trigger this requirement — non-residents can hunt unguided on private land with the proper licenses

**Application Deadlines (75 words):**
- Most draw applications open in January–February; deadlines typically in March–April
- Missing the draw window means waiting another year for limited quota species
- General licenses for some deer and antelope are available over the counter — no draw required
- Preference points accumulate even if you don't draw — building points is a long-term strategy

**Pro Tip (25 words):**

### **2. Wyoming Big Game Seasons: Elk, Deer, and Antelope Dates and Rules (425 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "wyoming hunting seasons," "wyoming elk hunting season," "wyoming deer hunting season," "wyoming elk regulations," "wyoming deer regulations," "wyoming antelope season dates," "wyoming big game hunting regulations"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Wyoming's big game seasons are what draw most out-of-state hunters. The state holds some of the highest elk and mule deer densities in the West, plus pronghorn antelope populations that offer unique high-volume hunting opportunities compared to other western states.

**Elk (125 words):**
- Archery: early season typically opens August 15 in select areas; general archery September 1–30
- General season: varies by hunt area, typically October through late November
- Limited quota (type 1) hunts require draw; general (type 2) licenses available OTC in some areas
- Non-resident elk license cost: significantly higher than resident (table recommended)
- Mandatory outfitter rule applies in wilderness hunt areas
- Best non-resident strategy: apply for limited quota early, use OTC general license as backup in non-wilderness areas

**Deer (125 words):**
- Mule deer dominate; whitetail in select eastern areas
- Archery: September 1–30 generally
- General season: typically October 1 through November in most areas
- Type 1 (limited quota) and Type 2 (general) licenses — most non-residents pursue Type 2 OTC
- Hunters may apply for maximum one general deer license per year
- General deer licenses valid in any hunt area open to general license holders — check hunt area maps on WGFD

**Antelope (100 words):**
- Archery season: August 15–September 30 (starts earlier than elk/deer, during heat — gear accordingly)
- General season: October into November
- Wyoming holds the largest pronghorn population in the US — more tag availability than other big game species
- Non-residents have a realistic shot at drawing limited quota antelope tags even in early preference point years

### **4. Wyoming Fishing Regulations: Non-Resident Licenses, Seasons, and What to Know (375 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "wyoming fishing regulations," "wyoming fishing license non-resident"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Wyoming's rivers, streams, and reservoirs hold some of the best trout fishing in the country — cutthroat, rainbow, brown, and brook trout — along with walleye, bass, and pike in lowland reservoirs. For non-residents planning a summer trip, fishing regulations are simpler than hunting but still require attention.

**License Types (100 words):**
- Non-resident annual fishing license (covers all license year — most economical for multi-trip visitors)
- Non-resident 1-day and 5-day licenses available for short trips
- Wyoming requires separate Conservation Stamp for all license holders
- Combination hunting/fishing licenses offer savings for hunters who also want to fish during their trip
- License year runs March 1 through the following February 28 — buy at the start of the trip season
- Fishing licenses are available online through WGFD or at licensed vendors statewide

**Season Dates and Rules (125 words):**

- Peak trout season: June through September — runoff typically clears by late June in most drainages
- Cutthroat trout: special regulations apply to protect native populations; catch-and-release required on some blue-ribbon waters
- Bag limits: typically 6 trout per day on general waters; reduced on designated blue-ribbon streams
- Fishing on private land: requires landowner permission even if accessing from a navigable stream in some circumstances — confirm water rights and access agreements before fishing private water

### **5. The Wyoming Draw System: How Non-Residents Compete for Limited Tags (375 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "wyoming hunting tags," "wyoming big game regulations," "wyoming elk seasons," "wy hunting regs"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
- Wyoming uses a preference point system for most limited quota big game species. Non-residents compete in a separate pool from residents — and with significantly higher fees. Understanding how the draw works, when to apply, and when to buy points vs. hunt is critical for long-term Wyoming success.

**How the Preference Point System Works (125 words):**
- One preference point earned per year of application without drawing
- Points increase your odds in future draws — the more points, the higher your chance at trophy units
- Non-residents compete against each other, not against residents, in the draw
- Wyoming also allows bonus points for purchase — separate from preference points
- Points are species-specific: deer points don't transfer to elk
- Strategy: buy points for your target species every year, even if you don't plan to hunt that year
- Some premium trophy units require 10–15+ points for non-residents; general units are achievable in 2–5 years

**What You Can Hunt Without Drawing (100 words):**
- Over-the-counter (OTC) general licenses available for certain deer and antelope units
- Small game, upland birds, and most waterfowl: no draw required
- Spring and fall turkey: typically OTC
- Private land hunting can shift the calculus — some landowners hold landowner tags (requires 160+ contiguous deeded acres within a specific hunt area), which may be sold or shared with guests through direct arrangements
- Note: LandTrust properties don't come with landowner tags, but the hunting access itself doesn't require a draw

**Pro Tip (50 words):**
"Apply for preference points the first year you're serious about Wyoming big game — even if you don't hunt that year. Every year without a point is a year of compounding you can never get back. The cost is minimal compared to the multi-year investment of a Wyoming elk hunt."
