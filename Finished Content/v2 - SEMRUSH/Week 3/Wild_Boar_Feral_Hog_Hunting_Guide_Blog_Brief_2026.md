### **1. Understanding Wild Boar and Feral Hog Biology (350–400 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "feral hog hunting," "wild boar vs feral hog," "wild hog behavior"

**Content Structure:**

**Wild Boar vs. Feral Hog vs. Wild Pig — What's the Difference? (75–100 words):**
- European wild boar (Sus scrofa) is the introduced ancestor; feral hog = domestic pig gone wild (same species, different history); "wild pig" is the catch-all
- For hunters: doesn't matter. Treat them the same tactically. Same behavior, same biology, same rules.
- Why it matters for regulations: Some states call them "wild boar" in legal language, some say "feral swine" — both refer to the same animal

**Behavior That Drives Your Hunting Tactics (175–200 words):**
- **Nocturnal:** Most active dusk to dawn — this is the single most important fact that should shape your strategy
- **Rooting behavior:** Follows food sources (corn, roots, acorns, small animals, garbage); find the damage, find the hog
- **Social structure:** Sows travel in sounders (groups of 3–30+); boars often solitary when mature. Shooting into a group vs. targeting a single animal requires different tactics
- **Senses:** Exceptional nose — wind direction is non-negotiable. Moderate eyesight. Can't distinguish red and green light (key for night hunting setup)
- **Aggression:** Hogs are dangerous when wounded or cornered — especially sows with piglets. A wounded hog that disappears into brush is a serious safety situation

**Where Hogs Live and Why They're Where They Are (75–100 words):**
- Cover: creek bottoms, river swamps, thick brush, transition zones between fields and timber
- Food drives location: Find fresh rooting damage in a corn or bean field, find the hog
- Water: Hogs won't be more than 1–2 miles from a reliable water source
- Sign to look for: Wallows (muddy depressions), rooting damage, tracks (look like overgrown deer tracks), rubs on fence posts

**Pro Tip (25–30 words):**
"Best scouting move: find the rooting damage, find the nearest wallow, drop a trail cam there, check it in 48 hours. You'll know exactly what you're dealing with."

### **2. The 5 Best Methods for Hunting Wild Hogs (450–500 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "best way to hunt boar," "how to hunt wild hogs," "hog hunting tips for beginners"

**Content Structure:**

**Opening (50 words):**
Most beginner guides pick one method and call it "the best." The honest answer: the right method depends on your terrain, your state's laws, and your budget. Here are five approaches, when each works, and what each costs.

**Method 1 — Spot and Stalk (75–90 words):**
- Best for: open terrain, ag fields, midday activity scouting
- Setup: quality binoculars required; approach from downwind; move slowly through terrain edges
- Best on: large Texas pastures, Oklahoma grain fields, open Southern hardwood flats
- Pros: no equipment cost; adaptable to changing hog location
- Cons: very difficult in thick swamp or river bottom brush; hogs spook fast at 100+ yards in the open

**Method 2 — Baiting (90–100 words):**
- Best for: first-timers; stand or blind hunting; confirming camera patterns before hunting
- Best bait: sour (fermented) corn is the gold standard — cheaper to make, draws from farther away than fresh corn; diesel-soaked corn (controversial but effective); commercial scents like Record Rack or Hog Wild
- Regulations note: Baiting is prohibited in some states even for invasive species — verify before setting up
- Pros: brings hogs to a predictable location; allows night setup; best for new hunters who need hogs to come to them
- Cons: 3–7 day establishment time; ongoing bait cost

**Method 3 — Calling (50–70 words):**
- Predator calls work because hogs are opportunistic omnivores — piglet distress sounds trigger aggression
- Short call bursts (30 seconds on, wait 2 minutes); hunt from a downwind position; be ready for fast movement
- Best for: when you have fresh sign but can't pattern movement to a specific spot

**Method 4 — Night Hunting (100–120 words):**
- The single highest-success method — hogs are most active at night, period
- Setup options: green/red LED feeder lights (budget — hogs can't detect these wavelengths), dedicated night vision scope, thermal monocular paired with IR illuminator
- Best setup for most hunters: thermal for detection, night vision for identification and shooting — the combination that the 12.31.25 LandTrust report flagged as a growing conversation cluster in Southern hunting forums
- Regulations: Legal for hogs in most Southern states on private land; check your state before hunting after sunset
- Note: See Section 3 for specific night hunting gear breakdown

**Method 5 — Dog Hunting and Helicopter (50–70 words):**
- Dog hunting: bay dogs + catch dogs — specialized skill; legal where dogs-on-game laws allow (primarily Southeast)
- Helicopter: Legal for aerial hog hunting in Texas; highly effective at population control; expensive ($500–$1,500 per session); guided operators licensed by TPWD
- Both methods require specific experience or licensed operators — not beginner territory, but worth knowing

**Pro Tip:**
"Combine methods for maximum results: bait a spot, confirm the pattern with a trail cam, then hunt it at night with a feeder light. That's the setup most experienced hog hunters default to — not because it's fancy, but because it works."

### **3. Hog Hunting Gear: What You Actually Need by Method (400–450 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "best caliber for hunting feral hog," "hog hunting gear," "night hog hunting"

**Content Structure:**

**Rifles and Calibers (125–150 words):**
- **Minimum effective caliber:** .243 or larger — hogs have a gristle shield (shoulder plate of dense cartilage) that defeats lighter rounds, especially on angled shots
- **Best calibers:** .308 (all-around workhorse), .30-06 (more common in deer states; works fine), 5.56/.223 (appropriate for younger hogs under 150 lbs; use quality expanding ammo), .300 BLK (subsonic for suppressed night hunting — quiet, effective inside 100 yards), 6.5 Creedmoor (for longer shots in open Texas terrain)
- **Semi-auto advantage:** When hunting sounders, semi-auto allows follow-up shots on a moving group; on a stationary single animal, bolt action is fine
- **Suppressors:** Legal in most hog-hunting states; drastically reduce report and allow follow-up shots without spooking the group

**Shotguns (40–50 words):**
- 12 or 20 gauge with slugs at close ranges (inside 75 yards)
- Buckshot is not reliable on hogs over 150 lbs — the gristle shield absorbs pellet energy; use slugs or sabot slugs for larger boars

**Archery (60–75 words):**
- Bow hunting hogs requires precise shot placement — quartering away into the forward shoulder area; avoid head-on angles entirely
- Minimum 50 lb draw; mechanical broadheads for penetration through the shoulder shield; lighted nocks for blood trailing in brush
- Reality check: hogs are tough and nocturnal — bow hunting them is the hardest method; not recommended as a first hog hunt

**Night Hunting Gear (100–120 words):**
- **Green or red LED feeder light:** Budget entry point ($30–$80); hogs can't detect these wavelengths; works surprisingly well inside 50 yards
- **Thermal monocular/scope:** Gold standard for detection — see heat signatures through brush in complete darkness; entry-level units (Pulsar Axion, AGM) now available $400–$800
- **Night vision scope:** Pairs with IR illuminator for identification and shooting; clearer target identification than thermal at mid-range
- **Cellular trail camera:** Remote checking without spooking the spot; worth the subscription cost on remote private land

**Other Essentials (40–50 words):**
- Quality waterproof boots for swamp and creek bottom terrain
- First aid kit — hog tusks cause serious puncture wounds, especially in dog-hunting contexts
- Hard-sided cooler (100 lbs+ of pork is worth recovering; hogs spoil faster than deer, especially in Southern heat)

### **5. Regulations, Licensing, and Safety (300–350 words)**

**Target Keywords:** "in what states can you hunt feral hogs," "do you need a license to hunt feral hogs," "hog hunting regulations"

**Content Structure:**

**Do You Need a License? (100–125 words):**
This is the most searched question around hog hunting — and the most misunderstood.

- **Texas:** No hunting license required on private land. License required on public land.
- **Florida:** No license required on private land with written landowner permission.
- **Georgia, Arkansas, Oklahoma:** State hunting license required, regardless of public vs. private land.
- **Federal law:** No federal regulations specifically govern feral hogs — they're not a federally protected species, and states manage them independently.
- **Bottom line:** Whether you need a license depends on two things: which state you're in, and whether you're on private or public land. Check your state wildlife agency before you go.

**Safety Rules for Hog Hunting (150–175 words):**
- **Don't approach a "dead" hog without checking:** A wounded hog that's still breathing will attack when approached — hog tusks cause serious puncture wounds and can be fatal
- **Wound shots are more dangerous than with deer:** Deer flee when wounded; hogs often charge. Know your shot before you take it.
- **Dog hunting:** Keep handlers back until bay dog has the hog controlled; use thick gloves and protective gear for catch dogs; never reach into brush blindly
- **Night hunting:** Positively identify your target before shooting. Know your backstop. A thermal scope helps enormously with target identification.
- **Field dressing:** Feral hogs can carry brucellosis — a bacterial infection transmissible to humans through blood contact. Wear disposable gloves every time; don't let blood contact open cuts or abrasions.
- **Meat recovery:** Hogs spoil faster than deer, especially in heat. Get them cooled as quickly as possible — bring a cooler with ice to the field.
