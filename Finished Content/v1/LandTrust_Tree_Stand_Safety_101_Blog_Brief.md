## **Blog Structure & Outline**

### **1. Introduction**
- Why treestand safety matters in 2025: falls remain a leading cause of hunting injuries; thousands occur annually (3k–6k+), many during ascent/descent. Reference key stats and seasonal reminders. (Mayo Clinic; Arkansas Game & Fish)
- Thesis: Follow a simple system—inspect, attach, climb, hunt, exit—using a full‑body harness + lifeline for every foot off the ground.
- Tie to LandTrust: safer hunts on private land with clear rules, owner guidance, and Field Notes that reduce risk and uncertainty.

### **2. When Falls Happen (and Why)**
- Climbing and transition moments are highest risk: entering/exiting stands and installing gear. (Hunter Ed)
- Common causes: improper/old gear, no FAS, poor three‑point contact, slippery footing, fatigue/drowsiness, permanent/homemade stands. (MN DNR; Bowhunter Ed)
- Snippet‑ready bullets: “Top 5 risk moments” and “Top 5 preventable errors.”

### **3. The Non‑Negotiable: Full‑Body Harness + Lifeline**
- Define Fall‑Arrest System (FAS): full‑body harness, tether with shock absorber, tree strap, suspension‑relief strap. (Hunter Ed; WI DNR)
- Golden rule: attach at ground level and remain attached until back on the ground. Keep tether short; don’t use chest/single strap belts. (WI DNR; MN DNR)
- Lifeline 101: prusik/carabiner connection from ground to seat; most accidents occur climbing, so continuous attachment is critical. (Mossy Oak)

### **4. Pre‑Season & Pre‑Climb Checklist (Snippet Target)**
- Inspect stands/hardware: check for rust, fatigue, loose bolts, worn straps; replace annually or per manufacturer guidance. (VA DWR)
- Choose the right tree: live, healthy, rough bark preferred; avoid rot/dead limbs/ash decline. (MD DNR; VT F&W)
- Fit and practice: adjust harness, practice at ground level, rehearse rescue plan and suspension‑relief strap use. (Hunter Ed)
- Pack a haul line, gloves, headlamp (red), phone/whistle; avoid sedating meds and alcohol. (GA DNR; Mayo Clinic)

### **5. Climb & In‑Stand Best Practices**
- Three‑point contact at all times on ascent/descent. (WI DNR)
- Use a haul line for bow/firearm; never climb with gear in hands/pack. (GA/MD DNR)
- Tether height: attach at/below head level when seated, with minimal slack to prevent long‑fall dynamics. (Hunter Ed)
- Suspension trauma awareness: deploy relief strap immediately; plan a self‑rescue method. (Hunter Ed)

### **6. Stand Types: Safety vs. Mobility**
- Ladder stands: most stable/easiest entry; great for new/returning hunters.
- Hang‑on & climbers: higher mobility; require more setup skill and strict lifeline/lineman’s belt use.
- Avoid permanent/homemade stands—degrade quickly; unsafe and damage trees. (Bowhunter Ed; MN DNR)
- Include a small comparison table (stability, setup complexity, ideal use, key safety add‑ons).

### **7. Shot‑Angle Safety from a Treestand**
- Aim considerations: bend at waist, avoid dropping bow arm; focus on exit path through vitals; avoid straight‑down shots. (Bowhunter; Mossy Oak)
- Angle‑compensating rangefinder and in‑season practice from elevation.
- Safety tie‑in: keep tether short enough to prevent overreach or balance loss during shots.

### **8. Exit Strategy Without Spooking Deer (and Staying Safe)**
- Pre‑planned egress route, wind‑aware, minimal light/red light; move slowly and quietly. (Bowhunter)
- If deer present and you cannot wait: use controlled diversions or a vehicle assist—then descend with three‑point contact.

### **9. FAQs (AEO/PAA Targets)**
- What is the biggest hazard of tree stands? — Falls during climbing/transition; improper equipment use. (Hunter Ed)
- When should a hunter wear a full‑body harness? — Any time feet leave the ground; attach at ground level until back down. (WI/MN DNR)
- What treestands are unsafe? — Permanent/homemade stands; use certified commercial stands. (Bowhunter Ed)
- What is the safest stand? — Ladder stands are generally more stable; regardless, a lifeline + full‑body harness is the critical factor. (Legendary Whitetails; Mossy Oak)
- At what height is fall protection required? — OSHA varies by industry (4–6+ ft), but hunters should use FAS any time off the ground. (OSHA overview; MN DNR/Hunter Ed for hunting)
- How many people fall each year? — Thousands (3k–6k+) annually; many cases underreported. (Mayo Clinic)
